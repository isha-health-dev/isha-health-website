# Real-World Outcomes of Telehealth-Delivered Oral Ketamine-Assisted Therapy for Depression and Anxiety: A Retrospective Analysis of 546 Patients

## Abstract

**Background:** Ketamine-assisted therapy has demonstrated rapid antidepressant effects in clinical trials, but real-world outcomes data from telehealth-delivered oral ketamine programs remains limited. This retrospective analysis reports clinical outcomes from a physician-led, at-home ketamine-assisted therapy program.

**Methods:** We analyzed de-identified clinical records from 546 unique patients treated through the Isha Health ketamine-assisted therapy protocol. Depression severity was assessed using the Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9), anxiety severity using the Generalized Anxiety Disorder-7 (GAD-7), and subjective well-being using a 1-10 self-report scale. Assessments were collected at regular intervals throughout treatment. The primary outcome was any reduction in PHQ-9 score from baseline in patients with moderate-to-severe depression (baseline PHQ-9 ≥ 10). Secondary outcomes included GAD-7 improvement, patient retention, and speed of response.

**Results:** Among patients with moderate-to-severe baseline depression (PHQ-9 ≥ 10), 88.79% demonstrated measurable improvement in PHQ-9 scores during treatment. Mean PHQ-9 scores decreased from 10.02 at baseline to 4.83 during treatment (mean reduction: 5.19 points; 48.2% reduction). Among patients with elevated anxiety, 92.64% achieved minimal anxiety levels (GAD-7 < 5) at some point during treatment. The average time to first measurable PHQ-9 improvement was 1.29 clinical assessments. Patient retention was 99.08%, with only 5 of 546 patients (0.92%) attending only a single session. Average treatment duration was 128 days.

**Conclusions:** Telehealth-delivered oral ketamine-assisted therapy was associated with clinically meaningful improvements in depression and anxiety symptoms across a large real-world patient population. The high retention rate and rapid speed of response support the feasibility and effectiveness of the at-home treatment model.

**Keywords:** ketamine, depression, anxiety, telehealth, oral ketamine, treatment-resistant depression, real-world outcomes, PHQ-9, GAD-7

## Introduction

Major depressive disorder (MDD) affects approximately 280 million people worldwide and remains a leading cause of disability (WHO, 2023). Despite the availability of multiple antidepressant medications, approximately one-third of patients with MDD do not achieve adequate response to first-line pharmacotherapy, a condition known as treatment-resistant depression (TRD) (Rush et al., 2006).

Ketamine, an N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptor antagonist, has emerged as a rapid-acting antidepressant with a mechanism of action fundamentally different from monoamine-based antidepressants. Multiple clinical trials have demonstrated rapid and significant reductions in depression scores following ketamine administration (Zarate et al., 2006; Murrough et al., 2013; Berman et al., 2000). The FDA approval of intranasal esketamine (Spravato) for TRD in 2019 further validated the therapeutic potential of ketamine-based treatments.

While the majority of published ketamine research has focused on intravenous administration in controlled clinical settings, there is growing evidence supporting the efficacy and safety of oral (sublingual) ketamine delivered via telehealth (Hull et al., 2022; Hassan et al., 2022; Mathai et al., 2024). This route of administration offers significant advantages in terms of cost, accessibility, and patient convenience, though published real-world outcomes data from telehealth oral ketamine programs remains limited.

This retrospective analysis reports clinical outcomes from 546 patients treated through the Isha Health ketamine-assisted therapy program, a physician-led telehealth practice offering personalized oral ketamine treatment for depression, anxiety, and PTSD.

## Methods

### Study Design
This was a retrospective analysis of de-identified clinical records from patients treated at Isha Health, a telehealth-based ketamine-assisted therapy practice operating in nine U.S. states (Arizona, California, Colorado, Florida, Georgia, New York, Oregon, Texas, and Washington).

### Participants
The analysis included 546 unique patients who received at least one clinical assessment during their treatment course. All patients underwent an initial 60-minute video consultation with a licensed physician, during which medical history, current symptoms, psychiatric history, and treatment goals were assessed. Patients were prescribed oral (sublingual) ketamine at individualized doses based on clinical judgment.

### Treatment Protocol
Patients received personalized treatment plans consisting of:
- Initial 60-minute physician consultation via secure video
- Individualized oral ketamine prescription sent to a licensed compounding pharmacy
- Medication delivered to the patient's home
- At-home ketamine sessions (approximately 2 hours per session) with detailed preparation instructions
- Follow-up consultations (30 minutes) for dosage optimization and progress monitoring
- Ongoing clinical support via secure messaging
- Coordination with the patient's existing therapist when applicable

### Outcome Measures

**Primary outcome:** Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) — a validated 9-item self-report measure of depression severity, scored 0-27. Scores are categorized as: minimal (0-4), mild (5-9), moderate (10-14), moderately severe (15-19), and severe (20-27).

**Secondary outcomes:**
- Generalized Anxiety Disorder-7 (GAD-7) — a validated 7-item self-report measure of anxiety severity, scored 0-21. Scores are categorized as: minimal (0-4), mild (5-9), moderate (10-14), and severe (15-21).
- Subjective well-being scale (1-10 self-report)
- Patient retention rate
- Speed of response (number of assessments to first measurable improvement)

### Definitions
- **Improvement:** Any reduction in PHQ-9 score from baseline during the treatment course
- **Moderate-to-severe depression:** Baseline PHQ-9 score ≥ 10
- **Minimal anxiety:** GAD-7 score < 5 achieved at any point during treatment
- **Single-session dropout:** Patient who attended only one session and did not return

### Statistical Analysis
Descriptive statistics were used to summarize patient demographics, baseline characteristics, and treatment outcomes. Mean scores and score changes were calculated across the treatment population.

## Results

### Patient Population
A total of 546 unique patients were included in the analysis. Approximately 1,900 PHQ-9 assessments and approximately 3,000 subjective well-being entries were collected across the study period.

### Depression Outcomes (PHQ-9)
Among patients with moderate-to-severe baseline depression (PHQ-9 ≥ 10):
- **88.79% demonstrated measurable improvement** (any reduction in PHQ-9 score from baseline)
- Mean baseline PHQ-9: 10.02
- Mean treatment PHQ-9: 4.83
- Mean PHQ-9 reduction: 5.19 points (48.2% reduction)
- This represents a shift from the "moderate depression" range to the "minimal symptoms" range

Among all patients (including those with mild baseline symptoms):
- Mean PHQ-9 reduction: 4.90 points overall
- Subjective well-being improvement: 3.55 points on a 10-point scale

### Speed of Response
Patients typically demonstrated their first measurable PHQ-9 score reduction by their second clinical assessment (mean: 1.29 assessments to first improvement). This is consistent with the rapid-acting mechanism of ketamine and compares favorably to the 4-6 week lead time typically required for SSRIs and SNRIs.

### Anxiety Outcomes (GAD-7)
Among patients with elevated baseline anxiety:
- **92.64% achieved minimal anxiety levels** (GAD-7 < 5) at some point during treatment
- This represents a transition from clinically significant anxiety to the minimal/subclinical range

### Patient Retention
- **99.08% retention rate** — 541 of 546 patients returned for follow-up after their initial session
- **0.92% single-session dropout rate** — only 5 of 546 patients attended a single session and did not return
- Mean treatment duration: **128 days** (~4.3 months)
- This sustained engagement suggests patients perceived ongoing benefit from treatment

### Subjective Well-Being
- Baseline stability: approximately 3,000 well-being entries showed stable baselines, confirming that improvements were treatment-associated rather than regression to the mean
- Mean improvement: 3.55 points on a 1-10 scale
- 56.25% of patients reported improvement of 100% or greater from baseline

## Discussion

### Principal Findings
This retrospective analysis of 546 patients treated with telehealth-delivered oral ketamine-assisted therapy demonstrates clinically meaningful improvements across multiple outcome measures. The 88.79% improvement rate in moderate-to-severe depression is notable and compares favorably to published clinical trial data, including the Murrough et al. (2013) randomized trial (response rate ~64% at 24 hours) and the Zarate et al. (2006) study (71% response, 29% remission at 24 hours).

The 92.64% rate of achieving minimal anxiety levels (GAD-7 < 5) suggests that oral ketamine may be particularly effective for comorbid anxiety, which is present in a majority of treatment-resistant depression patients.

The 99.08% retention rate is exceptionally high for a psychiatric treatment program and suggests that patients find the at-home telehealth model acceptable and the treatment beneficial. The average treatment duration of 128 days reflects patients who engage with the maintenance phase of treatment, not just the initial acute phase.

### Comparison to Existing Literature
Our findings are consistent with two large retrospective analyses of telehealth-delivered sublingual ketamine:
- Hull et al. (2022): 1,247 patients, 62.8% response rate, 32.6% remission rate on PHQ-9
- Mathai et al. (2024): 11,441 patients, antidepressant response in both non-severe (55.8%) and severe (18.1%) depression groups

Our higher improvement rate (88.79%) likely reflects our broader definition of improvement (any PHQ-9 reduction vs. 50% reduction) and our physician-led, personalized treatment approach with ongoing monitoring and dosage adjustment.

### Strengths
- Large real-world sample (N=546) representative of clinical practice
- Validated outcome measures (PHQ-9, GAD-7)
- Multiple assessment timepoints allowing longitudinal tracking
- High retention rate enabling meaningful follow-up data
- Treatment delivered in the patient's home environment, reflecting real-world conditions

### Limitations
This analysis has several important limitations:

1. **Retrospective design:** This is not a randomized controlled trial. Without a control group, we cannot attribute improvements solely to ketamine treatment. Regression to the mean, placebo effects, concurrent treatments, and natural disease course may contribute to observed improvements.

2. **Liberal improvement definition:** Improvement was defined as any PHQ-9 score reduction, which includes clinically modest improvements. This likely overestimates the rate of clinically significant response compared to the standard 50% reduction criterion.

3. **Self-report measures:** PHQ-9 and GAD-7 are self-report instruments subject to response bias, social desirability effects, and expectation effects.

4. **Selection bias:** Patients who seek out and pay for ketamine therapy represent a self-selected population that may differ from the general depression population in motivation, severity, and other prognostic factors.

5. **Missing data:** Not all patients completed assessments at every timepoint. Patients who dropped out or deteriorated may be underrepresented in the analysis.

6. **No long-term follow-up:** We report outcomes during the active treatment period (mean 128 days). Durability of improvements beyond the treatment course is not assessed.

7. **Single-site data:** All patients were treated through one practice, limiting generalizability.

## Conclusions

Telehealth-delivered oral ketamine-assisted therapy was associated with high rates of depression and anxiety improvement in a large real-world patient population. The combination of physician-led care, personalized dosing, validated outcome tracking, and a collaborative care model involving integration therapists may contribute to the favorable outcomes observed. Prospective controlled trials are needed to establish causal efficacy and long-term durability of these findings.
