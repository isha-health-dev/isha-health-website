After a ketamine session, something remarkable happens in your brain. Levels of brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) surge, new synaptic connections begin forming, and neural circuits that had been weakened by chronic depression start rebuilding. This period of heightened neuroplasticity, lasting roughly 24 to 72 hours after treatment, represents your best opportunity to turn temporary relief into lasting change.

For the science behind BDNF and ketamine, see our [deep dive on ketamine and neuroplasticity](/post/ketamine-bdnf-neuroplasticity). This post focuses on practical steps you can take to make the most of the window.

## Understanding the Window

Ketamine's antidepressant effects work through a cascade of molecular events. Within hours of treatment, the drug triggers a burst of glutamate signaling that leads to rapid increases in BDNF, a protein essential for neuronal growth and synaptic plasticity. BDNF activates the mTOR pathway, which drives the formation of new dendritic spines and synaptic connections, primarily in the prefrontal cortex.

This is not a permanent state. The window of peak plasticity begins within hours of your ketamine session and gradually closes over the following one to three days. During this time, your brain is unusually receptive to forming new neural pathways. What you do during this period can influence which pathways strengthen and which ones fade.

Think of it this way: ketamine provides the raw materials for brain renovation. What you build with those materials depends on your actions in the days that follow.

## Day of Treatment: The First 6 Hours

**Rest and reflect.** The hours immediately after your ketamine session are not the time for productivity. Your brain is processing the experience and beginning its neuroplastic response. Allow yourself to rest without screens or stimulation.

**Journal your experience.** While the session is fresh, write down any insights, emotions, or imagery that arose during treatment. These observations can become valuable material for therapy sessions later. For guidance, see our post on [intention setting for ketamine treatment](/post/intention-setting-for-ketamine-treatment).

**Hydrate and eat lightly.** Ketamine can cause mild nausea in some patients. Keep water nearby and eat a light, nutritious meal when your appetite returns.

**Avoid alcohol and recreational substances.** These can interfere with the neuroplastic processes unfolding in your brain and diminish the therapeutic benefit.

## Day 1 After Treatment: Active Engagement

**Schedule therapy.** If you work with a therapist, the day after ketamine treatment is an ideal time for a session. Research consistently shows that psychotherapy during the neuroplasticity window produces stronger and more durable results. Your brain is primed for cognitive restructuring, emotional processing, and the formation of new thought patterns.

**Move your body.** Moderate exercise, a 30-minute walk, yoga, or light cardio, has been shown to independently increase BDNF levels. Combining exercise with the ketamine-induced BDNF surge creates a compounding effect on neuroplasticity.

**Practice mindfulness or meditation.** Even 10 to 15 minutes of mindfulness practice can help consolidate the neural changes taking place. Meditation engages the prefrontal cortex and default mode network, the same regions where ketamine promotes synaptic growth.

**Engage in meaningful social connection.** Positive social interactions activate reward circuits and reinforce the emotional shifts that ketamine facilitates. Spend time with people who support your recovery.

## Days 2-3: Consolidation

**Continue journaling.** Track your mood, sleep quality, and any shifts in your thinking patterns. Many patients notice subtle but important changes during this period: reduced rumination, greater emotional flexibility, or a slightly brighter outlook.

**Maintain healthy sleep habits.** Sleep is when your brain consolidates new synaptic connections. Prioritize seven to nine hours of sleep during the neuroplasticity window. Avoid screens for an hour before bed, keep your room cool and dark, and maintain a consistent sleep schedule.

**Try something new.** Novelty stimulates neuroplastic processes. This does not need to be dramatic. Try a new walking route, cook a new recipe, listen to unfamiliar music, or pick up a creative hobby. Novel experiences give your newly forming neural pathways something constructive to encode.

**Limit stress where possible.** Chronic stress hormones like cortisol work against neuroplasticity. While you cannot eliminate all stress, try to reduce unnecessary stressors during this critical window. This might mean postponing difficult conversations, delegating tasks, or saying no to optional obligations.

## What to Avoid During the Window

- **Heavy alcohol use**: Alcohol suppresses BDNF and impairs synaptic plasticity.
- **Sleep deprivation**: Disrupted sleep undermines the consolidation of new neural connections.
- **Isolation**: While rest is important, complete withdrawal can reinforce depressive patterns.
- **Rumination**: If you find yourself spiraling into negative thought loops, use grounding techniques or reach out to your therapist.

## Building a Sustainable Routine

The neuroplasticity window is not a one-time event. Each ketamine session opens another window, and the habits you build during these periods can gradually become your baseline. Over the course of a treatment series, patients who consistently engage in therapeutic activities during their neuroplasticity windows report more substantial and lasting improvements.

## The Bottom Line

Ketamine opens the door. What you do next determines how far you walk through it. The 24 to 72 hours following each session represent a genuine biological opportunity. By scheduling therapy, exercising, journaling, sleeping well, and engaging in novel experiences during this window, you give your brain the best chance to build the new pathways that support lasting recovery.
