## Introduction

Music and psychedelics have a long history of being intertwined, from ancient rituals to modern therapeutic sessions. Many who have experienced psychedelics report that music takes on a deeper, more profound meaning, evoking intense emotions and altering perception in ways that are difficult to describe. But what exactly happens in the brain when psychedelics and music interact? Recent research is shedding light on the neuroscience behind this phenomenon, revealing how psychedelics enhance music perception and emotional processing in ways that could be beneficial for mental health and therapy.

‍

## The Neuroscience of Music and Psychedelics

Psychedelics such as LSD, psilocybin, and DMT primarily interact with the **serotonin 5-HT2A receptors**, which play a crucial role in cognitive flexibility, sensory processing, and emotional regulation. These substances alter brain activity by increasing connectivity between different regions, particularly within the **default mode network (DMN)** and the **limbic system**, which is responsible for emotions and memory.

Music, on the other hand, activates a variety of brain areas, including the **auditory cortex, limbic system, and prefrontal cortex**. When combined with psychedelics, these effects become amplified, leading to **heightened emotional responses, synesthetic experiences (seeing sounds as colors), and a deep sense of connection with the music**.

A study published in _Scientific Reports_ (2020) found that participants who listened to music while under the effects of psilocybin experienced **greater emotional depth and personal meaning** in the music compared to a placebo group. The study suggested that psychedelics may open up a ‘critical period’ in which the brain becomes more malleable, allowing for deeper emotional processing and new interpretations of sound.

‍

## How Psychedelics Change the Way We Perceive Music

1.  **Enhanced Emotional Response  
    **Music has a unique ability to trigger emotions, but under the influence of psychedelics, this effect is significantly amplified. Users often describe feeling an overwhelming sense of **awe, joy, sadness, or transcendence**, even with music they’ve heard before. This heightened emotional response may be due to increased activity in the **amygdala**, the brain’s emotional processing center.
2.  **Synesthesia: Seeing Sounds and Feeling Music  
    **Psychedelics can induce **synesthesia**, a condition where the senses blend together. Many users report **seeing colors and patterns that move in sync with the music**, or even feeling music as a physical sensation. This is thought to be caused by increased cross-talk between sensory regions of the brain.
3.  **Time Dilation and Deep Immersion  
    **Psychedelics can alter time perception, making a few minutes of music feel like an eternity. This **deep immersion** allows listeners to experience each note and nuance more profoundly, leading to a greater sense of connection with the music.
4.  **Increased Meaning and Insight  
    **Many people report that music under psychedelics takes on a **profoundly personal meaning**, often bringing up memories, emotions, or insights about their life. This effect is particularly powerful in psychedelic-assisted therapy, where carefully curated music playlists help guide patients through intense emotional experiences.

‍

### Music as a Tool in Psychedelic-Assisted Therapy

**‍**One of the most promising applications of the music-psychedelic connection is its role in **psychedelic-assisted therapy**. Studies on psilocybin therapy for depression and PTSD suggest that the right music can enhance the healing process by providing **emotional scaffolding**, helping patients process difficult emotions and experiences in a safe way.Clinicians carefully select **specific playlists** designed to match different phases of the psychedelic experience:

-   **Onset Phase:** Ambient, soothing music to ease into the altered state
-   **Peak Phase:** Deep, expansive music that encourages introspection and emotional release
-   **Integration Phase:** Grounding music that helps patients process insights and transition back to normal consciousness

A 2021 study in _Frontiers in Psychology_ found that participants undergoing psilocybin therapy rated music as one of the most crucial factors influencing their experience. The right music helped them feel supported and facilitated emotional breakthroughs.**The Best Types of Music for Psychedelic Journeys**Different genres and styles of music can evoke different effects when paired with psychedelics. Some of the most commonly recommended types include:

-   **Ambient and Electronic Music:** Artists like Brian Eno, Jon Hopkins, and Shpongle create atmospheric sounds that enhance introspection.
-   **Classical Music:** Composers like Bach, Beethoven, and Debussy provide rich, emotionally evocative experiences.
-   **Traditional and Shamanic Music:** Indigenous drumming and ceremonial chants, such as Icaros from Ayahuasca ceremonies, have been used for centuries to guide psychedelic experiences.
-   **Nature Sounds:** Ocean waves, rainforest ambiance, and bird calls can create a sense of deep peace and connection with the natural world.

## Can Psychedelics Change Musical Preferences?

Many people report that after a profound psychedelic experience, their musical tastes shift. Some find themselves more drawn to classical, spiritual, or instrumental music, while others develop a deeper appreciation for complex musical structures. This may be due to **changes in neural connectivity and emotional association with certain sounds**.A study conducted at Imperial College London found that participants who took psilocybin had a **sustained increase in appreciation for music**, particularly for genres they previously found uninteresting. This suggests that psychedelics may not only enhance music perception in the moment but create lasting changes in how the brain processes sound.
