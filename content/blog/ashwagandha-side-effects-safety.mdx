Ashwagandha has a reputation as a gentle, well-tolerated supplement, and for many people it is. But "natural" does not mean risk-free. Like any bioactive substance, ashwagandha can cause side effects, interact with medications, and pose specific risks for certain populations. Before you add it to your routine, here is what the evidence and clinical reports suggest you should know.

## Common Side Effects

In clinical trials, ashwagandha is generally well tolerated at standard doses (300 to 600 mg per day of a standardized root extract). However, some participants do report adverse effects, most of which are mild and gastrointestinal in nature.

The most frequently reported side effects include nausea, stomach discomfort, diarrhea, and, less commonly, vomiting. These tend to be more likely at higher doses or when the supplement is taken on an empty stomach.

Drowsiness and sedation are also reported, which is consistent with ashwagandha's proposed GABAergic activity. For some people this is a benefit, particularly if they take it in the evening. For others, daytime drowsiness can be an unwanted effect. If you experience sedation, adjusting timing or dose may help.

In rare cases, individuals have reported headaches, upper abdominal discomfort, or mild skin rash. Most of these resolve after discontinuation.

## Drug Interactions to Be Aware Of

Ashwagandha has the potential to interact with several classes of medication. If you take any of the following, consult your healthcare provider before starting ashwagandha:

- **Thyroid medications (levothyroxine, methimazole).** Ashwagandha may influence thyroid hormone levels, particularly T4 and TSH. Studies have shown it can increase thyroid hormone production, which could interfere with carefully calibrated thyroid medication dosing.
- **Sedatives and CNS depressants (benzodiazepines, sleep aids, alcohol).** Because ashwagandha may enhance GABAergic activity, combining it with other sedating substances could produce additive drowsiness or impairment.
- **Immunosuppressants (tacrolimus, cyclosporine, corticosteroids).** Ashwagandha has been shown to have immunomodulatory properties. In theory, it could counteract the effects of medications designed to suppress immune activity, though direct clinical data on this interaction is limited.
- **Diabetes and blood pressure medications.** Some evidence suggests ashwagandha may lower blood sugar and blood pressure, which could amplify the effects of these medications.

This is not an exhaustive list. If you are on any prescription medication, a conversation with your provider is the safest approach.

## Contraindications: Who Should Avoid Ashwagandha

Certain groups should avoid ashwagandha entirely or use it only under close medical supervision:

- **Pregnant or breastfeeding individuals.** Ashwagandha is classified as likely unsafe during pregnancy. Some traditional sources note it may have abortifacient properties, and there is insufficient safety data for use during lactation.
- **People with autoimmune thyroid disease.** Individuals with Hashimoto's thyroiditis or Graves' disease should be particularly cautious. Ashwagandha's thyroid-stimulating effects could worsen hyperthyroid states or complicate management of autoimmune thyroid conditions.
- **Those with hormone-sensitive conditions.** Because ashwagandha may influence hormonal pathways, individuals with hormone-sensitive cancers or conditions should discuss its use with an oncologist or specialist.

## Liver Toxicity Concerns

In recent years, a number of case reports have linked ashwagandha supplementation to liver injury, including several cases of clinically apparent hepatotoxicity. The mechanism is not well understood. It may involve direct toxicity of certain compounds, idiosyncratic reactions, or contamination of specific products.

A 2023 review in the journal *Hepatology* highlighted a growing number of reported cases and called for greater clinical awareness. While these events appear to be rare relative to the widespread use of ashwagandha, they are serious enough to warrant attention. If you experience symptoms such as jaundice, dark urine, persistent nausea, or upper right abdominal pain while taking ashwagandha, discontinue use and seek medical evaluation immediately.

## Choosing a Quality Supplement

The supplement industry is not regulated with the same rigor as pharmaceuticals. Product quality can vary significantly between brands, and contamination with heavy metals, pesticides, or undeclared ingredients is a documented concern.

To reduce risk, look for products that carry third-party certifications such as NSF International, USP (United States Pharmacopeia), or ConsumerLab verification. Choose standardized extracts like KSM-66 or Sensoril, which are the formulations most commonly used in clinical research and subject to more consistent quality control. Avoid products making exaggerated health claims or those lacking clear labeling of withanolide content.

## The Bottom Line

Ashwagandha is generally well tolerated, but it is not without risks. Side effects, drug interactions, and emerging liver safety concerns mean it deserves the same careful consideration you would give any other bioactive substance. Talk to your healthcare provider before starting, especially if you take medication or have a pre-existing condition.
