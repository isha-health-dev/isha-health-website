Expanded panels of urine drug screen checks often include ketamine assays since there are increased chances of misuse in psychiatric, emergency, and work environments where it may be dangerous. Immunoassay is an instrument that most rapid screens are based on because of ease and economy, but specificity is compromised, leaving the door open for cross-reactive interferences. 

‍

Quetiapine, a second-generation antipsychotic, is the only drug that consistently causes false-positive ketamine results in immunoassay screens. Despite foods’ notorious ability to bias other drug tests, evidence for dietary causes of ketamine cross-reactivity remains unavailable. Confirmatory analytical methods like gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS) or liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) are necessary to [determine ketamine exposure](/post/how-does-ketamine-work) from assay artifacts. These factors will help clinicians and individuals figure out testing with confidence and correctness. 

‍

## **Mechanisms Of False-Positive Ketamine Screens**

‍

Most false-positive ketamine results are due to cross-reactivity in immunoassay techniques, where ketamine imitates other compounds that are not ketamine. Interferences may arise from similar molecular shapes or functional groups that interfere with the assay's antibody binding. Knowledge of these mechanisms is essential for correctly interpreting drug test results and limiting inaccurate outcomes. 

‍

## **Immunoassay Cross-Reactivity**

‍

False positives occur when other non-ketamine molecules attach to assay antibodies designed for ketamine, thus giving a signal indistinguishable from true positives. This phenomenon, called cross-reactivity, is based on structural mimicry: Molecules with similar shapes or functional groups could spuriously activate the assay. Immunoassays differ from manufacturer to manufacturer, so the risk of cross-reactivity between test brands differs and may vary with changing assay formulations. 

‍

## **Structural Basis for Cross-Reactivity**

‍

Research shows that ketamine cross-reactants, both in ketamine and quetiapine sulfoxide, have an arylcyclohexylamine core or an adjacent nitrogen atom in a ring. In two Taiwanese case reports, patients treated with quetiapine showed positive ketamine immunoassay screens, as confirmed later by GC-MS. Even without an adequate understanding of mechanisms, structural homology seems sufficient for the quetiapine metabolites to bind ketamine antibodies. 

‍

## **Documented Reasons For False Positive Ketamine Results**

‍

False-positive ketamine results occur when other substances in the ketamine interfere with the ketamine testing process. The most written-about cause is quetiapine, a commonly administered antipsychotic that has structural similarities with ketamine, thereby inducing a cross-reaction. There are other possible causes, but they lack the same level of clinical evidence that would render confirmation testing very important in avoiding misinterpretation.  

‍

### **Quetiapine**

‍

Quetiapine is the only psychotropic drug known to show false positives on a ketamine screen based on specific confirmed cases. There are two clinical examples from Taiwan of quetiapine-treated individuals whose urine immunoassays read ketamine. An analysis through GC-MS later revealed the absence of ketamine, a more exhaustive review associated crossreactivity in ketamine assays with the three-ring structure and sulfoxide metabolite of quetiapine. Ketamine positives from patients on quetiapine should therefore be interpreted with care by clinicians, and confirmatory testing should be done as soon as possible.

### **Other Pharmaceuticals (Potential)**

‍

No other drugs are established to cause a ketamine cross-reactivity, but systematic data-mining has revealed many other unreported cross-interactions in different assays. While similar studies will focus on ketamine assays per se, quetiapine still rises as the only proven culprit. However, watchfulness for any new evidence calls for it.

## **Foods and Substances Affecting Urine Drug Screens**

‍

Even though these items do not produce false positives for ketamine up until now, they are known to interfere with other drug screening assays, and it is up for discussion on that note to understand the implications of testing for the broader array of potential substances. 

‍

Some foods, drinks, and bodily care products can cause irreversible results in urine drug screens due to their chemical components or metabolites that may react with antibodies involved in immunoassay. This underscores the need to be careful with dietary and lifestyle options before undergoing drug screening because such substances would influence the accuracy of other drug panels and produce false-positive findings. This knowledge guarantees a broader mindset in preparing and interpreting drug test results. 

‍

### **Poppy Seeds**

Poppy Seeds include trace opiates (codeine, morphine), which can push urinary opiate EIA results over the cutoff, but today’s thresholds are unable to limit the significant dietary impacts.

‍

### **Hemp Products and CBD**

‍

Hemp Products and CBD may contain ambiguous THC contents that will result in cannabinoid positives for some immunoassays.

‍

### **Quinine-Rich Beverages**

‍

Quinine-rich beverages such as tonic water have turned out to be associated with false-positive quinolone or opiate screens as a result of structural overlap. 

‍

### **Baby Wash And Supplements**

‍

Baby Wash and Supplements are among the drug groups that cross-react at times (miscellaneous drug panels), presenting us with a wide variety of interferences that can occur.

## **Detection Of Ketamine In Urine**

‍

The analysis of ketamine in urine is generally performed in two steps: a screening test and a confirmation test. Screening is usually performed by immunoassays, which are cheap and rapid but typically susceptible to false positives because of the cross-reaction of similar compounds. These preliminary trials give presumptive results that should be read cautiously in complicated clinical and legal situations. 

‍

### **Screening Vs. Confirmation**

‍

Nature of the first ketamine detection involves immunoassay screens valued for fast turnaround, but can be false positives. All positive immunoassay results are presumptive and necessitate further confirmation through a more specific method. 

‍

### **Confirmatory Testing**

‍

The confirmatory assays, GC-MS or LC-MS/MS, identify ketamine from cross-reactive analogues. They determine the compounds by chromatographic separation and confirm the mass spectra. These approaches almost wipe out false positives, leaving only valid ketamine exposures reported. Laboratories should have easy access to confirmatory testing and communicate the time frames to the subjects and clinicians. 

‍

## **Strategies To Minimize False-Positive Outcomes**

‍

Reducing false-positive ketamine findings necessitates a progressive and informed strategy. A good plan includes communicating clearly about the current medications, avoiding known cross-reactive substances, and performing confirmatory testing as necessary. These strategies guarantee the correct interpretations of drug screens, limiting the chances of erroneous outcomes and consequences. 

‍

### **Medication Disclosure**

‍

Never inform testing personnel of all the prescription or over-the-counter medications you are taking, particularly quetiapine, to guide interpretation. 

‍

### **Avoid Known Cross-Reactants**

‍

Even though no dietary components impact ketamine assays, avoiding poppy seeds, hemp, and quinine-rich beverages helps to avoid misreads in other panels. 

‍

### **Request Confirmatory Testing**

‍

In case an unexpected ketamine positive occurs, proactively request GC-MS or LC-MS/MS confirmation before taking any further steps. 

‍

### **Select Appropriate Assays**

‍

Institutions can use immunoassays with documented low cross-reactivity or updated formulations to correct previously reported interferences.

‍

## **Conclusion**  

‍

Extravirtually rare, but still of concern, false-positive ketamine findings can cause misdiagnoses, repeated interventions, and distress to patients. Quetiapine remains the only drug known to trigger ketamine cross-reactivity. It emphasizes the need for a complete medication disclosure and the need to base the test on confirmatory GC-MS or LC-MS/MS testing. ## **FAQs**

‍

### **How do you dispute a false positive drug test?** 

‍

If you want to contest a false positive drug test, ask for a more accurate confirmatory test such as GC-MS or LC-MS/MS. List medications, supplements, and recent dietary intake in detail to determine possible cross-reactants. 

‍

### **What can cause a false positive on a urine drug screen?**

Medications, some foods, or supplements may induce cross-reactivity with chemical structures similar to target substances, causing false positives. Common causes are antipsychotics, cold medications, or when poppy seeds are eaten in a harmful amount, depending on the drug being tested. 

‍

### **What supplements can cause a false positive drug test?** 

‍

Some supplements, such as those with ephedrine, hemp derivatives, or heavy doses of B vitamins, can result in false positives. Inconsistencies in the purity and labeling of the supplements increase the likelihood of chance results. 

‍

### **What is the most common false-positive drug test?** 

‍

Cannabinoid tests are among the most common false positives, with causes ranging from hemp products to drugs such as NSAIDs, among others. Cross-reactivity in the case of Immunoassays for THC is particularly strong because of the structural similarities of some of the compounds.
