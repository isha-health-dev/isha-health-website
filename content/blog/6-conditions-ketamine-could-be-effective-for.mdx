Lately, it feels like everyone in the medical community is talking about ketamine. After being synthesized in 1962 and used for over 50 years as a surgical anesthetic, ketamine has recently been recognized to be an effective treatment for depression and other mental health conditions. In this blog, we talk about six conditions that ketamine could be effective for.  

## Ketamine for Depression  

Ketamine has recently received a lot of attention as an alternative treatment for depression. While only esketamine nasal spray (Spravato) and injectable ketamine are approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for specific uses, other forms like oral or compounded ketamine are used off-label or in research settings. Unlike traditional treatments such as antidepressant medications and psychotherapy, which often take weeks to months before an improvement is seen, ketamine exerts its effect within days.

‍[Unlike traditional treatments such as antidepressant medications](/post/how-is-ketamine-different-from-traditional-antidepressants) and psychotherapy which often take weeks to months before an improvement is seen, [ketamine](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33994933/) exerts its effect within days. Most patients report an improvement in their mood within the first month of the treatment, although it may take a bit longer for some. Even among patients with treatment-resistant [depression](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30283029/), two thirds of them improve with ketamine treatment.

## Ketamine for Suicidality  

Ketamine has also been found to aid in the acute treatment of patients with acute suicidal behavior. This is an acute indication that requires close monitoring where in-person ketamine treatment is more appropriate than online treatment.  

## Ketamine for Anxiety  

In addition to mood disorders, chronic pain, and suicidality, ketamine also works well for [controlling anxiety](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022395614002337) from conditions such as [general anxiety disorder](https://academic.oup.com/ijnp/article/21/8/717/4989167?login=true) and [social anxiety disorder](https://www.nature.com/articles/npp2017194).    

## Ketamine for PTSD  

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder causes a set of anxieties, intrusive flashbacks, nightmares, and other painful memories of an extremely stressful experience. Although typically used to describe service people returning from war, PTSD can be observed in anyone who has undergone traumatic experiences. Certain stimuli viscerally remind a patient of the negative experience and can cause [a variety of symptoms](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6457782/) similar to depression and anxiety.

## Ketamine for Substance Use Disorder  

Ketamine has been found to help patients recover from opioid [addiction](/post/is-ketamine-addictive) by decreasing the pain and cravings associated with withdrawal. In addition to alcohol, researchers are currently examining ketamine’s efficacy in treating other addictive substances such as alcohol and cocaine, and the initial results are promising. It’s believed that [ketamine’s effect](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30140240/) is mediated through its ability to help your brain learn new behaviors [by forming new neural connections and pathways](/post/how-does-ketamine-work).  

## Ketamine for Chronic Pain  

Researchers have also found that ketamine is useful for helping patients with chronic pain. [In one study](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/acem.13755), ketamine was intravenously infused for 20 minutes and found to significantly reduce the pain reported by the study patients. It is hoped that repeat ketamine treatments would lead to long-term improvements by reprogramming the patient’s pain pathways. Most doctors currently rely on opioids to treat pain which can directly lead to [addiction](/post/is-ketamine-addictive) or drug-seeking behavior by patients. Ketamine would greatly reduce this behavior because it has a much lower risk of [addiction](/post/is-ketamine-addictive) and doesn’t provide the same ‘high’ that other drugs do.
